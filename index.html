Discover the History
of Batanes

Early History and Ivatan Culture (Pre-1783)

This era covers the arrival of the Austronesian ancestors around 4,000 years ago. The Ivatans developed a self-sufficient culture centered on farming, fishing, and trade along the Maritime Jade Road. They lived in fortified hilltop settlements called idjangs and created distinctive cultural artifacts and weather-resistant gear like the vakul. They maintained full autonomy until the late 18th century.

Spanish Colonial Era (1783–1898)

Formal colonization began in 1783 when the Spanish annexed the islands. The Spanish authorities consolidated the population from mountain fortresses into lowland towns and introduced limestone-and-mortar building techniques, which led to the iconic stone houses and churches seen today. The provincial capital, Basco, was established during this period.

American and Japanese Periods (1898–1945)

Batanes became a formal, independent province under American administration in 1909, during which public education and infrastructure were introduced. Due to its strategic northern location, Batanes was one of the first areas in the Philippines occupied by the Japanese Imperial Army at the start of World War II in 1941.

Contemporary Batanes (1946–Present)

Following liberation in 1945, Batanes was re-established as a peaceful province. In 1993, the area was protected as a protected landscape and seascape to preserve its unique culture and environment. The Ivatans are now known for their strong community integrity and preserved cultural heritage, attracting visitors interested in their unique lifestyle and natural beauty.
